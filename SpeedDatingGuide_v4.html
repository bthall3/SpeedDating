Section 1

Getting Ready for the Date

Our appearance is the first thing people notice about us. We can't change our physical appearance, but we can improve first impressions by looking clean and put-together. Speed-dating events are not formal, but we encourage you to look your best and wear something that makes you feel confident and comfortable. Consider outfits that are flattering to features you consider your "best."

Tip

Research suggests smell has an important influence on attraction. Be sure to have fresh breath and wear a little cologne or perfume if you have it.

Section 2

Pre-Date Nervousness

It is completely normal, and expected, to be a bit nervous before the speed-dating event. But thinking that we are nervous or anxious can make it difficult to relax and enjoy our conversations.

Excitement Reappraisal · Brooks (2014)

Instead of thinking "I'm nervous," reframe it as excitement. Research has shown that reframing anxiety as excitement can make people more relaxed and confident — both states involve high arousal, so the mental shift is easier than it sounds.

Try this

Repeat assuring phrases to yourself before the dates — "I am excited to meet new people" — and think of speed-dating as an opportunity rather than an evaluation.

Section 3

Beginning the Date — Introducing Yourself

Because speed-dating dates are "blind" dates, you will have to introduce yourself to each of your dates. This provides a great opportunity to start the date and guide the conversation. The following simple script provides a good template:

Person A "Hi, I'm [name], it's nice to meet you!"
Person B "Hi, [Person A], I'm [name], it's nice to meet you too!"
Why this works

Saying your date's name back to them (1) helps you remember it and (2) demonstrates that you were listening — a small but meaningful signal of interest.

At this point, unless Person B begins the conversation, Person A is on the hook to start. One good option: give Person B a genuine, specific compliment while saying their name.

Tip

Don't give every one of your dates the same compliment — they may overhear each other, and it will no longer feel special. Only give compliments you truly mean and make them specific to the person.

Section 4

During the Date — DOs and DON'Ts

Having a conversation with a complete stranger can be daunting. Below are research-informed suggestions for what to do and what to avoid.

✓  DO
Talk about more than demographics — share music taste, film preferences, hobbies, and notable experiences that signal your personality.Rentfrow & Gosling, 2006
Be playful and make jokes. Don't take the conversation too seriously.
Maintain open body language — relaxed posture, lean in slightly, smile, and make eye contact.
Ask follow-up questions. Depth over breadth — it's better to go deep on one topic than skim many.
Be authentic. Let your genuine reactions show rather than performing for your date.
✗  DON'T
Interview your date. Fire off questions without reciprocating. Aim for a 50-50 balance between talking and listening.
Complain about your life, exes, or classes. Negativity is consistently off-putting.Rozin & Royzman, 2001
Over-disclose or disclose too early. Mutual and gradual self-disclosure works best — jumping to deep secrets too soon backfires.
Bring up risky topics like politics, sex, or religion. Important to discuss eventually, but risky on a first date.
Humble brag. Subtly boasting about accomplishments often reads as inauthentic.Sezer et al., 2018

During the Date — Escalating Self-Disclosure

While small talk is natural in speed-dating settings, research shows that escalating self-disclosure meaningfully increases interpersonal closeness. The questions below are organized into three levels. Work through them in order (Level 1 → 2 → 3) and take turns answering.

Aron et al. (1997) · Sprecher (2021)

The fast friends paradigm has extensive empirical support: partners who engage in escalating self-disclosure report significantly greater closeness than those who stick to small talk.

Before you go

Pick one or two questions from each level to bring with you. Write them down — it's completely fine if you spend the whole date on just one. Depth of conversation is often better than breadth.

Tap ☆ to star questions you want to use.

Imaginative, low-stakes questions that open conversation without requiring vulnerability.

  • 1Given the choice of anyone in the world, whom would you want as a dinner guest?☆
  • 2Would you like to be famous? In what way?☆
  • 3Before making a telephone call, do you ever rehearse what you are going to say? Why?☆
  • 4What would constitute a "perfect" day for you?☆
  • 5When did you last sing to yourself? To someone else?☆
  • 6If you were able to live to the age of 90 and retain either the mind or body of a 30-year-old for the last 60 years of your life, which would you want?☆
  • 7Do you have a secret hunch about how you will die?☆
  • 8Name three things you and your partner appear to have in common.☆
  • 9For what in your life do you feel most grateful?☆
  • 10If you could change anything about the way you were raised, what would it be?☆
  • 11Take 4 minutes and tell your partner your life story in as much detail as possible.☆
  • 12If you could wake up tomorrow having gained any one quality or ability, what would it be?☆

Moderate questions inviting reflection on values, memories, and what matters most to you.

  • 13If a crystal ball could tell you the truth about yourself, your life, the future, or anything else, what would you want to know?☆
  • 14Is there something that you've dreamed of doing for a long time? Why haven't you done it?☆
  • 15What is the greatest accomplishment of your life?☆
  • 16What do you value most in a friendship?☆
  • 17What is your most treasured memory?☆
  • 18What is your most terrible memory?☆
  • 19If you knew that in one year you would die suddenly, would you change anything about the way you are now living? Why?☆
  • 20What does friendship mean to you?☆
  • 21What roles do love and affection play in your life?☆
  • 22Alternate sharing something you consider a positive characteristic of your partner. Share a total of 5 items.☆
  • 23How close and warm is your family? Do you feel your childhood was happier than most other people's?☆
  • 24How do you feel about your relationship with your mother?☆

Deep questions inviting genuine vulnerability and mutual self-disclosure.

  • 25Make 3 true "we" statements each. For instance, "We are both in this room feeling..."☆
  • 26Complete this sentence: "I wish I had someone with whom I could share..."☆
  • 27If you were going to become a close friend with your partner, please share what would be important for her to know.☆
  • 28Tell your partner what you like about them; be very honest this time, saying things that you might not say to someone you've just met.☆
  • 29Share with your partner an embarrassing moment in your life.☆
  • 30When did you last cry in front of another person? By yourself?☆
  • 31Tell your partner something that you like about them already.☆
  • 32What, if anything, is too serious to be joked about?☆
  • 33If you were to die this evening with no opportunity to communicate with anyone, what would you most regret not having told someone? Why haven't you told them yet?☆
  • 34Your house, containing everything you own, catches fire. After saving your loved ones and pets, you have time to safely make a final dash to save any one item. What would it be? Why?☆
  • 35Of all the people in your family, whose death would you find most disturbing? Why?☆
  • 36Share a personal problem and ask your partner's advice on how she might handle it. Also, ask your partner to reflect back to you how you seem to be feeling about the problem you have chosen.☆

Section 6

Ending the Date

At the end of each date — once the timer goes off — tell your date how much you enjoyed talking to them.

If you felt a connection, mentioning that you'd love to talk to them or see them again can be flattering and validating. It gives them permission to select you without feeling like they're risking rejection alone.
